## Project overview
Religion intersects with almost every domain of human life. As a result, concepts related to religious identities, practices, beliefs, and more are included within a wide variety of medical, social scientific, and humanities classification systems. For example, specific religious identities are codified in everything from Medical Subject Headings (MeSH) and the Systematized Nomenclature of Medicine -- Clinical Terms (SNOMED CT) through to the Library of Congress Demographic Group Terms (LCDGT) and Getty's Art & Architecture Thesaurus (AAT). Alongside these ontologies, a number of specialist taxonomies have also been developed, including several by the Association of Religion Data Archives (ARDA) and the Database of Religious History (DRH). At present, however, these vocabularies are mostly siloed within their respective disciplinary silos, and without broader consensus on either what they should contain or how they might map to each other. Further, only some of these classification schemes are available in Linked Open Data (LOD) format.

This application therefore exists to provide a way to explore how religion-related concepts are represented within major disciplinary ontologies--whether or not they yet exist as LOD. Building on an effort to reproducibly extract religion-related concepts from different source ontologies into a standardized dataset, this application allows you to explore that dataset in a variety of ways:
* **search**: explore how concepts are represented within specific ontologies and how to reference specific concepts as LOD
* **analyze**: investigate the relative prominence of concepts within and across different ontologies
* **browse**: explore how (if at all) concepts are semantically or hierarchically structured within different ontologies

You can also download the underlying data to develop your own analyses.

## Concept categorization and semantic scope
This project takes an intentionally broad view of the domain of religion. To aid exploring the thousands of instances of concepts across the source ontologies, therefore, each concept has been assigned a working category. While subject to change in no way intended to be authoritative, these categorizations broadly reflect the semantic intent of the source ontologies. The following categories are currently in use and are available as filters on the various exploration tools:
* **identities**: proper names of world religions, faith traditions, denominations, sects, cults, spiritual movements, or worldviews (e.g., Christianity, Anglican, Buddhist, Sunni, atheism)
* **beliefs**: religious or spiritual beliefs, doctrines, theological concepts, or objects of religious or spiritual beliefs such as supernatural beings or entities (e.g., beliefs about God, souls, afterlife, salvation)
* **practices**: religious or spiritual practices, rituals, activities, or behaviors (e.g., prayer, baptism, worship, service attendance, fasting, spiritual healing)
* **occupations**: religious or spiritual occupations, roles, or people (e.g., clergy, priest, imam, chaplain, shaman, monk)
* **buildings**: religious or spiritual buildings, physical structures, or infrastructure (e.g., church, temple, mosque, chapel, shrine)
* **communities**: religious or spiritual social structures, organizations, groups, or administrative bodies (e.g., congregation, parish, prayer group, diocese)
* **material**: religion-related or spirituality-related material concepts, physical objects, texts, or articles (e.g., Bible, font, vestment, rosary, prayer beads)
* **religious other**: religion-related concepts that do not fit one of the above categories
* **non-religious**: concepts that exist within specialist religion ontologies or within religion-related hierarchies in generalist ontologies but that are not inherently religious (e.g., materialism, ethics, attitudes toward abortion)

## Linked Open Data and concept identifiers
Established ontologies typically make use of the principles of Linked Open Data (LOD). This allows controlled vocabularies, taxonomies, and thesauri to be interlinked, read, and used by computers. A foundational requirement of LOD is that every concept must have a globally unique persistent resolvable identifier called a URI (Uniform Resource Identifier). For example, within the Library of Congress Demographic Group Terms (LCDGT), the religious group Zoroastrians have been assigned the URI `http://id.loc.gov/authorities/demographicTerms/dg2015060839`; data tagged with that URI tells humans and computers alike that it is in some way associated with the concept "Zoroastrians" as defined by the LCDGT. Because these URIs can be rather long, a standard shorthand called a CURIE (Compact URI) can also be used, which combines a recognized prefix corresponding to a base URL with a local concept ID (e.g., `LCDGT:dg2015060839`). While some sources are already using LOD format, many others (e.g., the ARDA or the Australian Standard Classification of Religious Groups) do not yet. As a result, this project makes use of synthetic CURIEs for these non-LOD sources so that every concept-source unit in the underlying dataset has a unique identifier--even if it does not have an associated resolvable address.

## Vision and goals
This application is part of a broader initiative to map religion-related concepts across ontologies, arrive at consensus metadata schemas for the domain of religion, and enable and promote the interdisciplinary findability, accessibility, interoperability, and reusability (FAIR) of  datasets that include religion-related data. For more about that initiative and to get involved, visit the associated [GitHub repository](https://github.com/njsgibson/religion-ontology-mapping).
